Our Proposal to reform New Zealand's property taxation

We propose the funding of councils by a land value tax, also called a location value tax, site valuation tax or the perfect tax. Land value taxes (LVTs) are levied as a fraction, usually very small, of the unimproved value of land - i.e. the value of the property minus the market value of any improvements like buildings. They improve economic efficiency, reduce inequality and wil reduce property prices by penalising land-banking and encouraging development. They are also fair: the value of unimproved land is attributable to its location and the society around it, not the work of its owners. LVTs have been successfully implemented throughout the world, including in parts of Australia and the United States as well as Denmark, Hong Kong and Lithuania. Put simply, they are the most intelligent way to tax property.

We are indifferent (although our members may not be) about whether the move to an LVT should be nationally-mandated or on a per-council basis, with central government encouragement. Some members of the Campaign support the expansion of LVTs so as to fund (partially or otherwise) central government, however the immediate position of the Campaign relates only to local government funding.

Notable proponents of LVTs

Please note: These people may or may not have endorsed the New Zealand Campaign for Intelligent Taxes specifically and nothing in this section should be understood as denoting such. They have, however, all endorsed the policy for which the NZCIT advocates: land value taxation.

Adam Smith

The founding father of economics, Adam Smith, wrote in his magnum opus 'the Wealth of Nations' that "nothing [could] be more reasonable" than a land value tax.

Jeremy Corbyn

The left-wing leader of the UK Labour Party went into the 2017 election promising a reform of council taxes, considering options like an LVT. Factions in his party like the Labour Land Campaign have long supported the policy.

Milton Friedman

The legendary free-market Nobel-winning economist, who advised Margaret Thatcher and Reagan and was an avowed anti-big government activist, called LVTs the "least bad tax."

Joseph Stiglitz

A Nobel laurete and former Chief Economist at the World Bank, Professor Stiglitz of Columbia has long been a radicial in favour of LVTs, contending they improve economic inefficiency and reduce inequality.

Sir Winston Churchill

The beloved British Prime Minister who led his country and her Empire through the Second World War supported LVTs, including making many speeches on the issue to the House of Commons.

John Kenneth Galbraith

A Post-Keynesian economist and long-time Democrat, Professor Galbraith of Harvard supported a land value tax, valuing it particularly for its progesivity and its economic efficiency.
