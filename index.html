My Philosophy of Government

I think the government solution to a problem is usually as bad as the problem and very often makes the problem worse.

Milton Friedman in An Economist's Protest

As so happens with Milton Friedman quotes, this one enscapulates my thoughts exactly: The fundamental problem with government intervention in both the market and private affairs is that no government official, no matter how intelligent, can understand each individual better than the individual himself. This problem is multiplied at government scale, where civil servants and parliamentarians make decisions effecting millions of people and families.

F. A. Hayek, another of my favourite economists and political philosophers, is one of the foremost experts on this issue. His essay, the Use of Knowledge in Society, in his verbose German style, discusses in more detail the issues with centralized planning and the comparative efficacy of market processes. It comes highly recommended from me - indeed, I believe it ought to be made required reading for all civil servants and Members of Parliament.

A Letter to the Editor of The Australian

I wrote the following letter, in my personal capacity, to the editor of The Australian, a conservative broadsheet paper, regarding the Citizenship Seven scandal and the judgment of the High Court of Australia thereon.

Sir,

I write in reference to the recent judgment of the High Court of Australia disqualifying the former Deputy Prime Minister and others from their posts in Parliament, in particular, the meaning of the term of “foreign power” in Section 44 of the Constitution.

The Court, under the precedent set by Sue v Hill, took this term to have its widest possible meaning: any power not the Commonwealth of Australia. In my view, this is a poor understanding of the term, that lacks an understanding of the distinction between Commonwealth realms and foreign states.

There is a reason that Commonwealth realms (for instance, New Zealand, Australia and the United Kingdom) do not have ambassadors but rather High Commissioners to the other realms. This is the same reason that the United Kingdom does not have a Foreign Office but rather a Foreign and Commonwealth Office and that a Commonwealth citizen, but not an EU citizen, can be a member of the British Parliament. There is a distinction between a fellow Commonwealth realm and a wholly foreign power.

The Court, under the precedent set by Sue v Hill, took this term to have its widest possible meaning: any power not the Commonwealth of Australia. In my view, this is a poor understanding of the term, that lacks an understanding of the distinction between Commonwealth realms and foreign states.

There is a reason that Commonwealth realms (for instance, New Zealand, Australia and the United Kingdom) do not have ambassadors but rather High Commissioners to the other realms. This is the same reason that the United Kingdom does not have a Foreign Office but rather a Foreign and Commonwealth Office and that a Commonwealth citizen, but not an EU citizen, can be a member of the British Parliament. There is a distinction between a fellow Commonwealth realm and a wholly foreign power.

Each realm shares a common monarch, who is the personification of the state. When two nations, even constitutional monarchies with differing political and legal systems, share a monarch, they are considered to be in a personal union and, not wholly independent from one another. Surely two states, personified by a single, shared inpidual, cannot be considered to be foreign to one another.

Thus, the High Court’s decision to disqualify the New Zealand and British citizens, including the former Deputy Prime Minister, of the Citizenship Seven under Section 44 of the Australian Constitution and the precedent set by Sue v Hill seems, with a wider understanding of the constitutional situation in the Commonwealth realms, counterintuitive and open to much debate.

Yours &c,


Mitchell W. Palmer

A Letter to the Editor of the New Zealand Herald

The following has been cross-posted from the New Zealand Powerhouse Institute

This letter was written and sent to the Editor of the New Zealand Herald by me, in my role as the Chief Executive of the New Zealand Powerhouse Institute, in response to Peter Malcolm’s article in the Friday 28th July Herald, titled “Make our worsening inequality an election issue.”

Sir,

I was disturbed on Friday to see your publication stoop to new lows by publishing a counterfactual article by Peter Malcolm which was comprised entirely of groundless assertions on the basis of mere anecdotes in complete disregard for findings of esteemed economists and other experts in the matter of economic inequality.

A good example of the many logical fallacies and simple fabrications in the article is the assertion in the title that inequality in New Zealand is“worsening.” In fact, the Gini coefficient for income (the standard metric for income inequality used by economists) both before and after taxes and transfers has remained relatively stable since the 1990s – with some minor volatility recently as a result of the Global Financial Crisis. There are many other examples littered throughout the article, however the limits of this format make it impossible for me to list and disprove them all.

I am frankly astonished that an article guilty of such crimes against logic and fact has been published in your newspaper and I hope a retraction is forthcoming.

Kind regards,

 

Mitchell Palmer
Chief Executive
New Zealand Powerhouse Institute
